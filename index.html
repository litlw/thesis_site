Progress Thus Far

My thesis as it stands right now is “how can consumers of popular mobile products be enabled and encouraged to question the products they buy, inform their buying habits, and eventually contribute to a consumer understanding of data privacy”. It’s long winded, but it’s also a complicated situation.

When I started I was interested in how Machine Learning was using data – and if there was a way to express how machine learning worked to an audience of non-data focused people. When I set out on that path, I realized that most of the sources I would have used (twitter, reddit, Instagram, and some news sources) have been shut down in the recent wake of GDPR and these data leaks. As a result, most API access has either been monetized or removed. I resorted to Canadian census data – but the response I got was that my data was not interesting or relevant to my thesis.

I tried to pivot to Data Privacy (the cause of my frustration) and began to investigate how data was kept private. I briefly looked into data anonymization and infrastructure supporting privacy vs privilege (adversary vs researcher). I thought my thesis was going to be an art piece to depict how data anonymization worked using images, and quickly realized that my idea was too abstract (and super complicated). After investigating Data Privacy methods, I moved to Data Privacy law and comparing China to Europe. In China, Ant Financial has produced a social product called Sesame Score. Sesame Score ranks people based on their social behavior for the benefit of banks to determine credit rating. Conversely, Europe had just created several new legislations. The most popular of Europe’s legislations was GDPR, while the most impactful was Chapter 9 (Provisions relating to specific processing situations). GDPR is an overall set of “rules and regulations” about data collection, management, use, and transfer.

What took me towards consumers and their understanding of GDPR and Ant Financial was a couple of articles. The first was a study of various people from the EU and their a) understanding of data privacy and b) willingness to do something to protect themselves. The overall study showed that while approximately 70% cared about data privacy, less than 41% did anything about it. The other article was about European countries moving to reduce the impact and coverage of GDPR.

Those articles set off red flags in my head and I realized that not everyone had spent the better part of four months studying data privacy – they probably only know what they see on the web. My new focus is a response to people resisting data privacy protection in the face of oncoming Social Scoring, data profiteering, and a loss of overall freedom.

I definitely want to focus my thesis in more, to do more research in terms of education and how to inform people, as well as forms of educational interaction.

  • Deibert, Ronald J. Black Code: Surveillance, Privacy, and the Dark Side of the Internet. Signal, 2013.

    Ronald Deibert, the head of Citizen Labs in Toronto, has a long and prolific history of involvement with data privacy. He and his lab were recommended to me for research by one of my advisors, and what I have learned from them and about them has been incredible even after just a few weeks of reading.

    Deibert’s book is a story of his lab’s journeys through the discovery and undoing of China’s ghostnet, as well as other journeys. On top of that, it is a critique and opinion piece from Deibert about data privacy overall. His book thus far has helped me position my thoughts in my own thesis. My direction of “What happens to you when you are compromised” as an introductory learning piece for everyday users is definitely inspired by what happened with the ghostnet story, as well as other stories that I read about in the news.

    The basic thesis is simple. Hackers rarely hack people for their identity – they hack for access, for mechanical resource, or for widespread intelligence gathering. The difference between access and widespread intelligence gathering is the precision with which a hacker might act. For access, a hacker would target someone specifically so that they can get closer to their true target. This often happens on a personal device or on a personal network of devices or area networks. Widespread intelligence gathering, on the other hand, is for the purpose of monitoring the consensus of devices over a very large network of people. This is often less invasive and also often less damaging to its victims. These two methods, however, are not mutually exclusive and can be part of the same strategy.

    Long story short – this article gave me focus.

  • Fairclough, Bradyn. "Privacy piracy: the shortcomings of the United States' data privacy regime and how to fix it." The Journal of Corporation Law, Winter 2016, p. 461.

    With all of these sources in mind, this article was enlightening and useful to ground many of my arguments and thoughts. The reason is many events that have taken place in the US and internationally in terms of data privacy are mentioned in this article. It also outlined a series of very concerning (and therefore good argument points) topics of US data privacy. I know this is a contentious topic and changes at any given moment, but as of 2019 these laws have not changed or been improved.

    As I write, an inquiry is going on for Facebook’s massive data breach that has pulled in google, apple, and amazon. It is likely that this massive inquiry will end with regulatory laws for these companies and these actions, but I cannot write about what has not happened yet. This article makes clear that the US has no data privacy law. Not only that, but its own constitution favors the wrongdoer in most situations – citing the second amendment.

    I think that lots of people don’t know this is the case for the US (and largely also Canada), but it definitely has an impact on whether a person would support GDPR or not. I think that if more people knew what was happening with their data, and how utterly unprotected they are, then they would act very differently.

  • Greenleaf, Graham. “The influence of European data privacy standards outside Europe: implications for globalization of Convention 108.” International Data Privacy Law, Volume 2, Issue 2, 1 May 2012.

    This article served as an incredibly thorough explanation and overview of GDPR and Convention 108. It is also an excellent resource of “what’s going on internationally” as a review of data policies at a very high level from several different countries. Before my change in thesis direction, I was interested in the comparison between GDPR and the changes coming in the banking world of China with respect to private data (trust me there’s something there).

    Something that became very clear in this article is what GDPR could become. Reading through the potential impacts and responses to Convention 108 in GDPR was an inspiring moment in my research. The basic summary of Convention 108 is similar to the security that countries have installed at their borders and their airports. A complex system that checks for security risks and international threats as people enter the country. By simply changing some of the words around of that description, you have the gist of Convention 108. For instance: A complex system that checks for security risks and threats as users enter the national web space.

    There are other insights in this article, but Convention 108 is by far the most impactful and has the loudest resonance both for and against in respective communities.

    Leading up to this article, I had generated a serious amount of interest in users’ perspective of data privacy. I already had done loads of research about GDPR, ANT financial, and various other players on the international stage. The overall story was already becoming clear to me – which is naïve but that’s how it felt. This article, however, opened it up when it presented the stats about the users in Europe and their perspective on Data Privacy and GDPR.

    The basic thesis of this article was; Is the EU, and potentially also the world, moving towards a user centric internet with freedoms and protections similar to those of national legislation by way of digital regulation? The arguments stem from adoption of GDPR and perception of GDPR. One of those arguments really sticks to the core of my thesis – do people support GDPR?

    It is made clear through field research that people do not really know what’s going on with data privacy – but they do care about data privacy. What was also clear was that the exchange of goods or services for a person’s data was a very uneven scale. The average user is / was giving away their precious identifiable data in exchange for nearly insignificant conveniences on their mobile devices.

  • The New York Times. “How Cambridge Analytica Exploited the Facebook Data of Millions | NYT.” Youtube, The New York Times, April 9, 2018, https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mrnXv-g4yKU.

    In an effort to keep up with the current times, I often turn to New York Times and Globe and Mail for resources. This video in particular covers the events that took place over an agreement between Cambridge Analytica and Facebook. This event, which is still shaking the world with respect to data privacy, is exactly what I want to combat. The entire documentation that New York Times provides – while 100% accurate – is indicitave of what people think is the real end point of data privacy being compromised. There is some discussion in the other academic work of the adversary and the researcher as two ethical poles of people who need data.

    This article discusses neither of them in depth, and simply says “oops we spilled your data”. Cambridge analytica poses itself to be a researcher – benign and in the interest of overall social growth – while in reality we have no idea whom else might have wanted to purchase data from that company – by way of the university research lab.

    I want to dissuade people from thinking a facebook data leak is the real risk. It is not. The real risk is what people might do to accomplish their nefarious goals – and what they might use you for in the process.
